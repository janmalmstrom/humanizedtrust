Microsoft Sentinel vs Splunk: SIEM for NIS2 Compliance

Updated 19 April 2026 · Read time: ~8 minutes · Audience: CEO, CFO, Board
Summary
  • A SIEM tool is mandatory for NIS2 compliance under Ireland's S.I. No. 322 of 2024.
  • The two market leaders — an on-premises tool costing €150k+ and a cloud-native alternative — offer very different tradeoffs.
  • NIS2 doesn't prescribe which SIEM you buy. It prescribes what you detect, log, and report.
  • The real cost isn't the software. It's the operational overhead, integration work, and personnel.
The Bottom Line

An expensive SIEM on-premises doesn't protect you from NIS2 fines if it's not integrated, not monitored, and not actively hunting threats. A lean, cloud-native SIEM that's properly tuned outweighs a sprawling on-premises deployment that sits idle. Choose based on your threat model and incident response capability — not on brand reputation alone.

You've received a €150,000 quote for a SIEM tool. Your IT director swears by it. Your Irish incident response team is asking why they need yet another platform to log into. Your CFO is asking whether the expensive option actually prevents the €10 million NIS2 fines NCSC Ireland has warned about.

This isn't a software preference question. It's a compliance and operational risk question. And the answer depends on what you're actually trying to detect.

Why a SIEM is mandatory for NIS2

Under S.I. No. 322 of 2024 (Ireland's NIS2 Regulations), essential entities are required to implement specific security measures, including:

  • Incident detection and monitoring — you must be able to identify when something is wrong
  • Activity logging across systems — you must know what happened, when, and by whom
  • 24-hour incident notification to NCSC Ireland — you can't report what you don't see
  • 72-hour detailed incident report — you need proof of what you observed

A SIEM (Security Information and Event Management) tool is how you do all of this. It ingests logs from your network, servers, databases, firewalls, and applications. It correlates events, identifies anomalies, and alerts you when something looks suspicious.

Without a SIEM, you can't meet these requirements. With the wrong SIEM — one that's misconfigured, unmonitored, or over-engineered for your environment — you've spent the money without gaining compliance.

A common mistake: Buying a SIEM, deploying it, and assuming your security is solved. NCSC Ireland will check whether your SIEM is actually alerting on the right events. Having a tool installed is different from using it to detect threats.

The two contenders: On-premises vs. Cloud-native

When you're evaluating a SIEM investment, you're usually comparing:

On-premises SIEM (€150k+ licensing, plus infrastructure)

A traditional on-premises SIEM tool requires:

  • Upfront capital cost: €150,000–€500,000 for licensing
  • Hardware: Servers, storage, networking equipment
  • Deployment: 3–6 months to configure and integrate with your environment
  • Operational overhead: A dedicated SIEM engineer (or two) to maintain, tune, and respond to alerts
  • Scalability challenge: Log volumes grow; you'll need to expand storage and processing capacity every 18–24 months
  • Upgrade risk: Major version upgrades require downtime and reconfiguration

When this makes sense: If you have multiple terabytes of log data per day, a large security operations center (SOC), or regulatory requirements that mandate on-premises log retention, an on-premises SIEM may justify itself.

Cloud-native SIEM (€50k–€100k annually, pay-as-you-grow)

A cloud-native SIEM is hosted by the vendor. You pay per gigabyte of log ingestion or per user. It includes:

  • Lower upfront cost: Annual subscription, no capital expenditure
  • Automatic scaling: Handles traffic spikes without infrastructure investment
  • Built-in integrations: Pre-configured connectors to common platforms (Microsoft 365, AWS, Azure, etc.)
  • Faster deployment: Weeks, not months
  • Vendor-managed updates: No downtime, no version jumps
  • Shared SOC knowledge: The vendor's threat research informs your alerts out of the box

When this makes sense: If you have a lean security team, variable log volumes, or you're migrating to cloud infrastructure (Microsoft 365, Azure, AWS), a cloud-native SIEM reduces operational friction.

What NIS2 actually requires from your SIEM

Neither tool is "NIS2-compliant" out of the box. Compliance depends on how you configure and use it. NCSC Ireland will audit:

1. Detection of the right events

Your SIEM must trigger alerts on:

  • Unusual login attempts or privilege escalation
  • Mass data access or exfiltration
  • Malware indicators or command-and-control traffic
  • Denial-of-service patterns
  • Configuration changes to critical systems

An out-of-the-box SIEM won't know your "normal." You must tune the rules and baselines to your environment. A cloud-native SIEM often includes vendor-managed rule sets based on global threat intelligence. An on-premises SIEM puts tuning on you.

2. Immutable log retention

NIS2 requires that incident logs cannot be deleted or modified — even by administrators. Both tools support this, but you must configure it. NCSC Ireland will verify it's enabled.

3. Integration with your incident response process

Your SIEM must be connected to your incident response team. That means:

  • Alerts go to a monitored inbox or on-call system
  • Escalation procedures are documented
  • Incident response playbooks reference the SIEM for evidence

A €150,000 SIEM that nobody checks is worse than a €50,000 SIEM that actively alerts a trained responder.

4. Audit trail of the audit trail

You must be able to prove that your SIEM alerts were reviewed and acted upon. This means SIEM audit logs, incident tickets, and response documentation must all connect. Both on-premises and cloud tools support this, but it requires operational discipline.

Case Study: Irish Financial Services Company

Organisation: 280-person fintech regulated by the Central Bank of Ireland (CBI) — classified as an essential entity under NIS2.

Challenge: They had purchased an on-premises SIEM three years earlier for €200,000. It was deployed but severely underutilized. Alerts were firing at a rate of 50,000 per day. The security team was drowning in noise and ignoring most alerts. NCSC Ireland initiated a routine audit in Q4 2025.

Finding: The SIEM existed, but there was no evidence of active monitoring. Alert logs showed no human review for 47 days straight. The tuning was so poor that the tool was generating more noise than signal.

Outcome: CBI issued a formal notice requiring remediation within 90 days. The company brought in a SIEM consultant (€40,000), re-tuned the rules (2 months of work), and integrated alerts into their incident response ticketing system. They also hired a part-time SIEM analyst (€60,000/year). Total remediation cost: approximately €160,000 over the first two years — more than the on-premises license itself.

Lesson: The tool doesn't matter. The process matters. They could have avoided this with a €60,000 cloud-native SIEM and one analyst from day one.

Integration costs: The hidden expense

When you request a SIEM quote, vendors typically quote only the license. They don't quote integration. That's where real costs hide.

On-premises SIEM integration

  • Network connectivity: Firewall rules, VPN access, SSL certificates
  • Log forwarding setup: Configure each application server to send logs to the SIEM (syslog, WinRM, API calls)
  • Parser development: If your legacy applications use non-standard log formats, you'll write custom parsers
  • Performance tuning: As log volume grows, you optimize storage, retention policies, and indexing
  • Estimated cost: €50,000–€150,000 and 3–6 months of your team's time

Cloud-native SIEM integration

  • API authentication: OAuth tokens, service principals, API keys
  • Pre-built connectors: Most cloud-native SIEMs have out-of-the-box connectors to Microsoft 365, Azure, AWS, Salesforce, etc.
  • Custom integrations: For legacy systems, you may still write custom API calls or log forwarders
  • Estimated cost: €20,000–€60,000 and 2–4 weeks (due to pre-built connectors)

If you're already in Microsoft 365 and Azure (which many Irish enterprises are), a cloud-native SIEM with native Microsoft connectors saves months of integration work.

The Irish context: NCSC expectations

NCSC Ireland has published guidance on what incident detection and logging should look like under NIS2. Their position is clear: the tool doesn't matter; the outcome does.

When NCSC auditors arrive, they ask:

  • "Show us the last intrusion you detected. How long between first alert and human response?"
  • "How many alerts did your SIEM generate last month? How many did you investigate?"
  • "Which events trigger a critical alert? Who reviews those alerts?"
  • "Can you produce a log entry from 6 months ago without modification?"

They don't care whether you answer using an on-premises or cloud SIEM. They care that you have answers.

Common regulatory trap: NCSC Ireland often finds that organizations have deployed a SIEM but have not integrated it with their incident response process. The tool generates alerts, but the alerts go to an unmanned inbox. This fails the 24-hour notification requirement under Article 23 of NIS2.
