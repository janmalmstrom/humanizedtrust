NIS2 Mandatory Security Measures: The Complete Requirements List

Updated 19 April 2026 · Read time: 8 minutes · Audience: CEOs & Board Members
Summary
  • Article 21 of NIS2 mandates 10 specific security measures for essential and important entities in Ireland.
  • Each measure has documented evidence requirements — auditors will ask to see them.
  • Compliance is not optional; the NCSC Ireland and sector regulators inspect for these measures regardless of whether an incident has occurred.
  • Your board must approve and document implementation of each measure under your personal responsibility (Regulation 32, S.I. 322/2024).
Critical Point

Article 21 is not a checklist of "best practices." It is a legally binding list of 10 security domains you must document, implement, and be prepared to demonstrate to auditors. Your board's inability to produce evidence of these measures is itself a sanctionable breach.

The NIS2 Regulations (S.I. No. 322 of 2024) require Irish essential and important entities to implement 10 mandatory security measures. These are not suggestions. They are statutory obligations backed by fines up to €10 million for essential entities and €7 million for important entities.

What many boards don't yet understand is that the NCSC Ireland and sector regulators (CBI for finance, ComReg for telecoms, CRU for energy, etc.) are already conducting inspections. They are looking for documentation of these 10 measures. If the documentation is absent, you are in breach — even if no cyberattack has occurred.

The NCSC Ireland is active. Notification of the competent authority is mandatory within 72 hours of a serious incident. Regulators use these notifications as entry points for full audits of your security posture. Plan to be inspected.

What Are the 10 Mandatory Measures?

Article 21 of the NIS2 Directive (transposed in Ireland as S.I. 322/2024) requires:

Measure Focus Area
1. Risk Assessment & Management Identify, assess, and document all cyber risks
2. Incident Handling & Reporting Detect, respond, and report incidents within legal timelines
3. Business Continuity & Disaster Recovery Maintain operations and recover data after disruptions
4. Supply Chain & Third-Party Security Manage risks from vendors, suppliers, and external services
5. Security by Design & Development Build security into systems from inception
6. Asset Management & Inventory Know what you own, where it is, and who accesses it
7. Access Control & Authentication Restrict who can access systems and verify their identity
8. Cryptography & Data Protection Encrypt sensitive data at rest and in transit
9. Physical & Environmental Security Protect facilities, hardware, and infrastructure
10. Personnel & Awareness Training Train staff and manage human security risks

Below, we break down each measure in plain language, explain what auditors will look for, and outline a minimum viable implementation.

Measure 1: Risk Assessment & Management

What It Means

You must formally identify all cyber risks to your organisation — from external threats (ransomware, data theft) to internal weaknesses (poor access controls, outdated software). This assessment must be documented, reviewed at least annually, and approved by the board.

What Auditors Look For

  • A written risk assessment covering all IT systems, data, and business processes
  • Evidence that risks have been scored (e.g., high/medium/low)
  • A mitigation plan for each significant risk
  • Board minutes showing approval of the risk assessment and mitigation strategy
  • Annual review and update of the assessment

Minimum Viable Implementation

Conduct a risk assessment workshop with IT, operations, and business leaders. Document: the scope of systems covered, the risks identified, their likelihood and impact, and assigned owners for mitigation. Present findings to the board with a written recommendation for approval. Schedule annual review dates and maintain a log of updates. Cost estimate: €3,000–€8,000 for external consultant-led assessment, or internal staff time if expertise exists.

Measure 2: Incident Handling & Reporting

What It Means

When a serious incident occurs — data breach, system compromise, ransomware infection — you must detect it, contain it, investigate it, and report it to the NCSC Ireland within strict legal timelines. The reporting timeline is: 24-hour early warning, 72-hour full notification, 1-month final report.

What Auditors Look For

  • An incident response plan with roles, responsibilities, and escalation procedures
  • A defined list of what constitutes a "serious" incident (impact thresholds)
  • Evidence of incident detection capabilities (logs, alerts, monitoring)
  • A contact list for NCSC Ireland notification with backup contacts
  • Template for incident reporting and communication
  • Training records showing staff understand escalation procedures

Minimum Viable Implementation

Draft an incident response plan (template available from NCSC Ireland website). Assign an incident lead, a technical investigator, a communications lead, and a legal lead. Define "serious incident" thresholds (e.g., >500 records affected, >4 hours downtime, >€50k impact). Store NCSC Ireland contact details in a secure, accessible location. Conduct a tabletop exercise simulating an incident to test the plan. Cost: €2,000–€5,000 for plan development; ongoing: staff time for drills and updates.

Critical timeline: Missing the 24-hour early warning window or 72-hour notification deadline is itself a sanctionable breach, separate from the underlying incident. Build margin into your reporting process.

Measure 3: Business Continuity & Disaster Recovery

What It Means

Your organisation must be able to recover from a major disruption (cyberattack, data loss, facility damage) and restore critical business functions within defined timeframes. You must have backup systems, documented recovery procedures, and regular testing.

What Auditors Look For

  • A documented business continuity and disaster recovery plan (BCDRP)
  • Identification of critical business functions and their recovery time objectives (RTO) and recovery point objectives (RPO)
  • Backup strategy with documented retention periods
  • Evidence of backup testing (logs, test results, dates)
  • Disaster recovery testing records (at least annually)
  • An off-site or geographically separated backup location

Minimum Viable Implementation

Identify your 5–10 most critical business processes. Define acceptable recovery times (e.g., email within 4 hours, financial systems within 8 hours). Set up automated backups to a geographically separate location (cloud provider meets this requirement). Test restore procedures quarterly. Document results and remediate any gaps. Board approval of RTO/RPO targets and annual review of recovery test results. Cost: €1,000–€3,000 for planning; backup infrastructure cost varies (often included in cloud subscriptions).

Measure 4: Supply Chain & Third-Party Security

What It Means

Every vendor, cloud provider, outsourced service, and contractor that touches your systems or data creates a risk. You must evaluate their security practices, define contractual security requirements, and monitor their compliance.

What Auditors Look For

  • A vendor inventory listing all third parties with access to systems or data
  • Security assessment questionnaires (SAQs) or audit reports from critical vendors
  • Contracts that include security clauses (incident reporting, breach notification, audit rights)
  • Evidence of due diligence — interviews, reviews, certifications (ISO 27001, SOC 2)
  • A risk rating for each vendor (high/medium/low)
  • Monitoring schedule for ongoing compliance

Minimum Viable Implementation

Create a spreadsheet of all vendors with system or data access (cloud, IT support, payroll, legal, consulting, telecommunications). For "critical" vendors (those with high system or data access), request a security assessment or audit report. Review vendor contracts for security clauses; add them if missing. Rate each vendor by risk. Schedule annual re-assessments. Board awareness of critical vendor risks and mitigation. Cost: €1,500–€4,000 for initial assessment and vendor outreach; ongoing: time for monitoring.

Measure 5: Security by Design & Development

What It Means

If your organisation develops custom software, operates cloud applications, or manages significant IT systems, security must be built in from the start — not bolted on as an afterthought. This covers secure coding, testing, and deployment practices.

What Auditors Look For

  • Secure development lifecycle (SDLC) policies and procedures
  • Security requirements in design specifications
  • Code review and vulnerability scanning practices
  • Testing protocols (unit tests, integration tests, security tests)
  • Change management procedures (approvals before deployment)
  • Patch management policy and implementation log

Minimum Viable Implementation

If you develop software, adopt a secure SDLC framework (e.g., OWASP or NIST guidelines). Mandate code reviews before production deployment. Use automated vulnerability scanning tools in the build pipeline. Document your patch management process: who approves patches, how quickly they're deployed, how they're tested. Train developers on secure coding principles. Cost: €2,000–€6,000 for process development and tooling; ongoing: tool subscriptions and training.

Measure 6: Asset Management & Inventory

What It Means

You must know what IT assets you own, where they are located, who is responsible for them, what data they hold, and who has access to them. This includes servers, laptops, mobile devices, software licenses, databases, and cloud services.

What Auditors Look For

  • A comprehensive IT asset register or CMDB (configuration management database)
  • Assets classified by criticality and sensitivity
  • Owner assigned to each asset
  • Location, status (active/retired), and end-of-life date documented
  • Access logs showing who can access each asset
  • Regular reconciliation (at least annually) of inventory against actual assets

Minimum Viable Implementation

Build a spreadsheet (or use free IT asset management tools) listing all servers, workstations, network devices, software, and cloud services. Include owner, location, criticality, data classification, and decommissioning schedule. Reconcile against actual systems annually. Identify and classify sensitive assets (payment systems, customer databases, HR data). Document who has access and why. Cost: €1,000–€2,500 for tool setup and initial inventory; ongoing: staff time for updates.

Measure 7: Access Control & Authentication

What It Means

Not everyone should be able to access everything. Access must be granted on a "need-to-know" basis, regularly reviewed, and revoked when it's no longer needed. Authentication (proving who you are) must be strong — ideally multi-factor.

What Auditors Look For

  • Access control policy (principle of least privilege)
  • User access lists for critical systems, segregated by role
  • Approval process for granting access (manager sign-off required)
  • Regular access reviews (at least semi-annually)
  • Deprovisioning procedure (access removed on termination or role change)
  • Multi-factor authentication (MFA) enabled for remote access and privileged accounts
  • Logs of access grant/revoke activity

Minimum Viable Implementation

Document your access control policy: define roles (admin, user, guest), specify who can approve access, and set access review cycles. Implement MFA for all remote access (VPN, email, cloud applications) and for any privileged admin accounts. Use a directory service (Active Directory, Entra ID) to centrally manage user identities and access. Schedule quarterly or semi-annual access reviews by business managers. Maintain logs of access changes. Cost: €2,000–€5,000 for policy development and MFA implementation; ongoing: staff time for reviews.

Measure 8: Cryptography & Data Protection

What It Means

Sensitive data (customer information, financial data, trade secrets, personal data) must be encrypted when stored ("at rest") and when transmitted ("in transit"). Encryption keys must be securely stored and managed.

What Auditors Look For

  • A data classification policy (public, internal, confidential, restricted)
  • Identification of what data is sensitive and where it is stored
  • Encryption in use for all confidential and restricted data (at rest and in transit)
  • Key management procedures: who holds keys, how they're stored, rotation schedules
  • Evidence of encryption implementation (configuration checks, certificate audits)
  • Data retention and destruction policy (how long is data kept, how is it securely deleted)

Minimum Viable Implementation

Classify your data by sensitivity. Enable encryption for all cloud storage (AWS S3 encryption, Azure encryption, Google Cloud encryption). Enforce HTTPS/TLS for all web applications and data in transit. Use Full Disk Encryption (BitLocker, FileVault) on all laptops and workstations. Implement database encryption for any on-premises databases holding sensitive data. Define key rotation schedules (annually or per industry standard). Document your data retention policy and implement automated deletion of expired data. Cost: €1,500–€4,000 for encryption tool setup; cloud providers often include encryption at no extra cost.
