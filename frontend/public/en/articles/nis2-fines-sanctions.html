NIS2 Fines and Sanctions: What Your Organisation Risks

Updated 17 April 2026 · Reading time: approx. 8 minutes · Audience: CEOs, Board Chairs, Board Members
Summary
  • For CEOs and board members in NIS2-regulated sectors who want to understand the real sanction exposure under Irish law.
  • Fines can reach up to €10 million or 2% of global annual turnover — whichever is higher.
  • Regulation 32 of S.I. No. 322 of 2024 creates direct personal liability for management body members.
  • The NCSC Ireland is already active. The window to treat this as a future agenda item has closed.
Key Takeaway

NIS2 is not an IT problem — it is a personal liability issue for you as CEO or board member, with fines of up to €10 million and the risk of a professional ban if your organisation falls short.

The NIS2 Directive is now Irish law. S.I. No. 322 of 2024 — the European Union (Measures for a High Common Level of Cybersecurity) Regulations 2024 — came into force in October 2024. It applies to your organisation right now, regardless of whether you have read it. And it carries consequences that no board can afford to ignore: administrative fines of up to €10 million, supervisory orders that can halt your operations, and — the part most CEOs do not expect — personal liability for individual members of the management body.

This article gives you a straight account of the sanction regime: how it works, what triggers an investigation, how fines are calculated, and what a realistic worst-case scenario looks like for a mid-size Irish company. It is written for the boardroom, not the server room.

The NCSC Ireland is already conducting supervisory activity. Ireland's National Cyber Security Centre (ncsc.gov.ie) is the primary competent authority under S.I. No. 322 of 2024. Sector regulators — including the Central Bank of Ireland (CBI), ComReg, and the Commission for Regulation of Utilities (CRU) — are also empowered to act. The first formal enforcement actions across the EU are already underway. Waiting is no longer a neutral choice.

What the Law Actually Says

NIS2 is an EU directive. In Ireland, it was transposed through S.I. No. 322 of 2024, which came into operation in October 2024. The Regulations apply to organisations operating in 18 critical sectors — from energy, transport, and banking to digital infrastructure, healthcare, food production, manufacturing, and research.

Two categories of entity are defined:

  • Essential entities — large organisations with a systemically critical role (generally 250 or more employees, or annual turnover exceeding €50 million and a balance sheet above €43 million), operating in high-criticality sectors.
  • Important entities — medium-sized organisations in regulated sectors (generally 50 to 249 employees, or turnover between €10 million and €50 million).

Ireland has a specific dimension that makes this particularly acute: a large number of multinational companies — including major tech firms, pharmaceutical groups, and financial services organisations — are registered in Ireland and may qualify as essential entities with cross-border obligations across the EU. If your Irish-registered entity is the EU headquarters for a larger group, your NIS2 obligations may extend beyond your Irish operations alone.

If you are uncertain whether your organisation is captured, the cost of finding out is far lower than the cost of guessing wrong.

The Fine Structure — In Plain Terms

The sanction regime under S.I. No. 322 of 2024 is graduated. The maximum fine depends on whether your organisation is classified as an essential entity or an important entity. In both cases, the applicable fine is whichever is the higher of the two figures — the fixed ceiling or the percentage of global turnover.

Category Maximum Fine Alternative Ceiling
Essential Entity €10,000,000 2% of global annual turnover
Important Entity €7,000,000 1.4% of global annual turnover

Consider what this means in practice. An Irish-registered essential entity that is part of a global group with €2 billion in annual turnover faces a potential fine of up to €40 million — because 2% of €2 billion exceeds the €10 million fixed ceiling. The global turnover figure is used, not just the Irish revenue. For multinationals headquartered here, that number can be very large indeed.

Important: Fines are assessed per infringement, not as a single aggregate cap for the organisation. A company can face simultaneous sanctions for failing to implement required security measures, for failing to report an incident on time, and for failing to demonstrate management oversight — each as a separate matter. The exposures compound.

Beyond Fines: The Full Range of Sanctions

Administrative fines are the most visible sanction, but they are not the only one. The NCSC Ireland and sector regulators have a broader toolkit at their disposal, and understanding each element is important for any board risk assessment.

Supervisory Orders

The competent authority can issue binding instructions requiring your organisation to take specific remedial actions within a defined timeframe. These are not suggestions. Failure to comply with a supervisory order is itself a separate infringement, which carries its own sanction. A supervisory order can require you to commission an independent security audit, submit to on-site inspections, or implement specific technical and organisational measures — regardless of the cost or disruption involved.

Temporary Operational Restrictions

For essential entities, the competent authority can impose a temporary suspension of certification or authorisation relevant to the services provided. In practical terms, this can mean a temporary ban on operating a specific service until compliance is demonstrated. For any organisation whose business model depends on continuous digital service delivery, this is potentially more damaging than a fine.

Public Disclosure

The authority can require public disclosure of the infringement — effectively naming the organisation and the nature of the failing. In an environment where enterprise clients, institutional investors, and regulators scrutinise cybersecurity posture, the reputational consequences of a public disclosure can far exceed the financial cost of the fine itself.

Personal Liability for Management Body Members

This is the element that changes the risk calculus for every CEO and board member in scope. Regulation 32 of S.I. No. 322 of 2024 provides that members of the management body of an essential or important entity can be held personally liable for infringements of the Regulations where those infringements result from a failure of the management body to fulfil its obligations.

The Regulations require management bodies to:

  1. Approve the cybersecurity risk management measures taken by the organisation
  2. Oversee the implementation of those measures
  3. Undertake regular training to maintain sufficient knowledge and skills to identify and assess cybersecurity risks and their impact on the organisation's services

Where these obligations are not met, the competent authority can — in serious cases — impose a temporary ban on an individual from exercising managerial functions in the organisation. This is not a fine paid by the company on your behalf. It is a professional prohibition directed at you personally. It cannot be indemnified by the organisation, and it follows you — not the company.

Personal consequence: Regulation 32 is not theoretical. It creates a direct line of accountability from a cybersecurity failure to you as a named individual. A boardroom minute that says "cybersecurity delegated to IT" does not discharge this obligation. The law requires the management body to actively approve, oversee, and demonstrate competence. Delegation without oversight is not a defence.

What Triggers an Investigation?

Understanding the enforcement pathway is as important as understanding the penalties. Investigations do not only follow high-profile incidents. The NCSC Ireland and sector regulators can initiate supervisory activity in several ways:

1. A Reportable Incident That Is Not Reported

The incident reporting timeline under S.I. No. 322 of 2024 is strict. A significant incident must generate an early warning within 24 hours, a full incident notification within 72 hours, and a final report within one month — all submitted to the NCSC Ireland. Organisations that experience a breach and attempt to manage it quietly, without notifying the NCSC, face a double exposure: a sanction for the underlying security failure and a separate sanction for the failure to report. The reporting obligation is not contingent on the outcome of the incident. It is triggered by the incident itself.

2. Routine Supervisory Activity and Audits

The competent authority does not need to wait for an incident to initiate a review. Proactive supervisory inspections — including document requests, on-site audits, and technical assessments — are explicitly provided for. Essential entities are subject to more intensive supervision than important entities, but both categories can be reviewed at any time. An organisation that cannot produce its security documentation within the timeframe requested — typically 14 to 30 days — is already in a difficult position before the substantive review has even begun.

3. Third-Party Complaints

A supervisory review can be triggered by a complaint from an employee, a customer, a business partner, or another EU member state authority. Ireland's position as home to the EU headquarters of many multinational firms means that a NIS2 complaint originating in another member state can land directly with the NCSC Ireland for investigation. Cross-border NIS2 mechanisms make this a realistic and increasingly common scenario.

4. Identified Vulnerabilities or Public Incidents

Media coverage of a significant service disruption, a data breach notification under GDPR, or intelligence shared between competent authorities can also initiate supervisory activity. The NCSC Ireland operates within a network of EU cybersecurity authorities. Information flows in both directions.

How Fines Are Actually Calculated

The maximum figures — €10 million or 2% of global turnover — are ceilings, not defaults. In practice, the competent authority weighs a range of factors when determining the actual fine:

  • Severity and duration of the infringement — a one-off procedural gap is treated differently from a systemic, prolonged failure
  • Nature of the harm caused — whether the infringement resulted in actual disruption to services or data compromise
  • Intentional or negligent conduct — deliberate concealment of an incident is treated more seriously than a genuine reporting error
  • Previous infringements — repeat failures attract higher sanctions
  • Degree of cooperation with the authority — organisations that engage transparently and remediate promptly are treated more favourably
  • Financial capacity of the organisation — sanctions are calibrated to be genuinely deterrent, not merely a cost of doing business

The practical implication is clear: an organisation that has documented its security measures, reported an incident promptly, and cooperated fully with the NCSC Ireland is in a categorically different position from one that has done none of these things. The fine for the same underlying security failure can differ by an order of magnitude depending on how the organisation has behaved before and after the event.

What Counts as Adequate Compliance?

The NCSC Ireland does not assess whether you tried hard. It assesses whether you can demonstrate that the required security measures exist and function. Good intentions, verbal assurances, and informal practices carry no weight in a supervisory review. Documentation does.

The security measures required under Article 21 of the NIS2 Directive — and reflected in S.I. No. 322 of 2024 — cover ten domains. Each must be addressed with documented policies, processes, and evidence of implementation:

  • Risk analysis and information system security policies
  • Incident handling — detection, response, and recovery
  • Business continuity and crisis management
  • Supply chain security — including your relationships with direct suppliers and service providers
  • Security in network and information systems acquisition and development
  • Policies and procedures to assess the effectiveness of security measures
  • Basic cyber hygiene practices and cybersecurity training
  • Policies on the use of cryptography and, where appropriate, encryption
  • Human resources security and access control policies
  • Use of multi-factor authentication and secure communications

Beyond these technical and organisational measures, the management body must demonstrate its own engagement. Board minutes approving the security programme, records of management training, and documented oversight of implementation are the evidence that protects both the organisation and its individual directors from the most serious sanctions.

Illustrative Scenario

Scenario: Mid-size Irish technology services company, 160 employees, classified as an important entity under S.I. No. 322 of 2024.

In January 2026, the company experiences a ransomware attack. The IT manager identifies the intrusion within hours and begins containment. The CEO is briefed but decides to defer formal notification to the NCSC Ireland pending an internal assessment of the full scope of the incident. The 24-hour early warning deadline passes. The 72-hour notification deadline passes. The company notifies the NCSC on day nine, once the incident is substantially contained.

The NCSC initiates a supervisory review. During the review, it emerges that the company had no formally adopted incident response plan, that the board had never approved the company's cybersecurity risk management measures in a documented meeting, and that no cybersecurity training had been recorded for board members or senior management.

Outcome: The NCSC issues a supervisory order requiring immediate remediation across all three areas within 90 days. An administrative fine is levied for the failure to report within the statutory timeline. A second fine is levied for the absence of required security documentation. The CEO receives a formal notice under Regulation 32 that personal liability has been engaged, with a warning that a temporary management ban remains under consideration pending the outcome of the remediation period.

Total direct financial exposure — fines, legal costs, and the cost of emergency compliance remediation: estimated at €800,000 to €1.4 million. Indirect costs — client attrition, contract renegotiations, and reputational impact — are additional and unquantified.

Frequently Asked Questions

Has anyone actually been fined in Ireland yet?
S.I. No. 322 of 2024 came into force in October 2024 and the NCSC Ireland is now conducting supervisory activity. Formal enforcement actions under NIS2 have already been issued in other EU member states, and Ireland's enforcement framework is fully operational. Organisations that are waiting to see a headline before acting are already behind the curve — and are likely accumulating the exact compliance gaps that attract the highest sanctions.
We're a relatively small company — do the fines really apply to us?
Size matters for classification, but not in the way many assume. The threshold for an "important entity" is 50 employees or €10 million in annual turnover within a regulated sector. If you are above those thresholds in one of the 18 NIS2 sectors, the full sanction regime applies — the same maximum fines, the same personal liability provisions, the same incident reporting obligations. There is no reduced penalty tier for smaller organisations within scope. The distinction between essential and important entities affects the ceiling, not the principle.
Can't we just delegate all of this to our IT department or CISO?
Delegation of technical implementation is entirely appropriate. But Regulation 32 of S.I. No. 322 of 2024 is explicit: the obligation to approve, oversee, and demonstrate competence in cybersecurity risk management rests with the management body — not with the CISO or the IT team. If the NCSC initiates a review, they will ask to see board-level decisions, not just technical configurations. The CEO and each board member must be able to show they understood the risks, approved the measures, and monitored implementation. That is a governance obligation, not a technical one.
What if we wait another six months before starting the compliance work?
Enforcement does not wait for your internal planning cycle. Neither do cyber incidents. Every day that passes without documented security measures in place is a day on which an investigation — or a breach — can find you fully exposed. Fines are assessed against your compliance posture at the time of the review, not against what you had planned to do. Starting a gap analysis today costs a fraction of what it costs to respond to a supervisory notice with no documentation in place.
Our company is registered in Ireland but operates across multiple EU countries. How does that affect our NIS2 obligations?
Ireland's role as EU headquarters for many multinationals makes this a particularly important question. Under NIS2, an entity is generally regulated by the competent authority of the member state in which it is established. For Irish-registered entities, that means the NCSC Ireland is your primary supervisory authority. However, the security and incident reporting obligations apply to your operations across all EU member states. A significant incident affecting your operations in Germany or France, for example, may trigger notification obligations to the NCSC Ireland even if the incident originates elsewhere. Cross-border coordination mechanisms between EU competent authorities mean that issues identified in other member states can and do reach the NCSC Ireland.
Does NIS2 apply even if we have never had a security incident?
Yes, completely. Compliance under S.I. No. 322 of 2024 is about having the right governance, processes, and documentation in place — not about your incident history. The NCSC Ireland can initiate a supervisory review of your organisation without any incident having occurred. If your organisation cannot demonstrate that it has implemented the required security measures under Article 21, that gap is itself a sanctionable infringement, regardless of whether you have ever been breached. A clean incident record is not a defence against a compliance failure.
