NIS2 72-Hour Incident Notification: Complete Compliance Guide

Updated 17 April 2026 · Reading time: approx. 9 minutes · Audience: CEOs, Board Chairs, Board Members
At a Glance
  • For CEOs and board members of NIS2-regulated organisations who need to understand their incident reporting obligations under Irish law.
  • The 72-hour notification to the NCSC Ireland is mandatory — not optional, not discretionary.
  • Missing the deadline is itself a sanctionable offence, separate from the incident that triggered it.
  • Management body members can be held personally liable under Regulation 32 of S.I. No. 322 of 2024.
  • Penalties for essential entities reach up to €10 million or 2% of global annual turnover.
Key Takeaway

When a significant cyber incident hits your organisation, you have 72 hours to notify the NCSC Ireland with a structured report. The clock starts from the moment you become aware — not when the legal team finishes reviewing the situation. Waiting is not a strategy. It is a second offence.

A ransomware attack locks your systems at 9 a.m. on a Tuesday. By Wednesday morning, your IT team has confirmed it is a significant incident. You call an emergency board meeting for Thursday. By the time you sit down, the 72-hour window under Ireland's NIS2 Regulations has already closed — and you have not yet filed a single notification to the NCSC Ireland.

That sequence of events is not hypothetical. It is the pattern that regulators across the EU are already identifying in post-incident reviews. And in Ireland, under S.I. No. 322 of 2024 — the statutory instrument that transposed NIS2 into Irish law — failing to notify within the prescribed timeframes is a sanctionable infringement in its own right, entirely separate from any liability arising from the incident itself.

This guide gives you exactly what you need to understand your obligations, prepare your organisation, and — if the worst happens — respond correctly from the moment the clock starts ticking.

The NCSC Ireland is active. The National Cyber Security Centre (ncsc.gov.ie) has been empowered as the primary competent authority for NIS2 in Ireland since October 2024. Sector regulators including the Central Bank of Ireland (CBI), ComReg, and the Commission for Regulation of Utilities (CRU) are also conducting oversight in their respective sectors. Supervisory activity is already underway.

The Three-Stage Reporting Timeline

NIS2 — and by extension S.I. No. 322 of 2024 — establishes a structured, three-stage notification process. Each stage has a distinct deadline and distinct information requirements. Think of it as a progressive disclosure: you are not expected to have all the answers immediately, but you are expected to start reporting immediately.

Stage Deadline Purpose Recipient
Early Warning 24 hours Alert the authority — minimal information required NCSC Ireland
Incident Notification 72 hours Structured report with initial assessment NCSC Ireland
Final Report 1 month Full analysis, root cause, remediation steps NCSC Ireland

The 24-hour early warning is intentionally minimal. Its purpose is to ensure the NCSC Ireland is aware that something significant has occurred. You are not required to have a full technical picture — but you must have enough information to flag that the incident meets the threshold of significance. We will define that threshold in the next section.

The 72-hour incident notification is where most organisations struggle. It requires an initial assessment of the incident's nature, scope, likely impact, and any cross-border implications. It does not need to be perfect — but it does need to be submitted on time and in good faith.

The one-month final report is the comprehensive document: root cause analysis, the full timeline, the remediation actions taken, and any lessons learned. This is where your documentation discipline over the preceding weeks will either protect you or expose you.

What Is a "Significant Incident"?

Not every cyber event triggers the notification obligation. The NIS2 Regulations apply to significant incidents — a defined term with specific criteria. Understanding this definition is critical, because mistakenly believing an incident is below the threshold does not excuse a missed notification deadline.

Under NIS2, an incident is considered significant if it has caused — or has the potential to cause — severe operational disruption or financial loss to the entity, or if it has affected — or could affect — other natural or legal persons by causing considerable material or non-material damage.

In practical terms, the NCSC Ireland and sector regulators will look at the following indicators when assessing significance:

  • The number of users or services affected by the disruption
  • The duration of the incident — how long systems were unavailable or compromised
  • The geographic spread — including whether other EU member states or critical infrastructure in other countries is implicated
  • The extent of data loss or data exposure, particularly where personal data or critical operational data is involved
  • Whether the incident has caused or is likely to cause reputational, financial, or safety-related harm
  • Whether the attack vector used is novel, sophisticated, or could indicate a coordinated campaign

A ransomware attack that encrypts your core business systems for 18 hours? Almost certainly significant. A phishing email that was caught by your filters before delivery? Almost certainly not. A phishing email that was clicked, credentials were stolen, and an attacker had access to your network for four days before detection? That is where your incident response team and legal counsel need to make a rapid, documented assessment — because the answer is almost certainly yes.

Irish context — cross-border significance: Many organisations registered in Ireland — particularly in technology, financial services, and pharmaceuticals — operate as EU headquarters for multinational groups. If an incident at your Irish entity has potential consequences for affiliated entities or customers across other EU member states, this is a relevant factor in the significance assessment. The NCSC Ireland may share notification information with competent authorities in other member states. Your incident response plan must account for this cross-border dimension from day one.

What Must the 72-Hour Notification Actually Contain?

This is where many organisations fail — not because they miss the deadline entirely, but because they submit a notification that is so incomplete it triggers an immediate follow-up investigation from the NCSC Ireland. A vague notification is not a safe notification.

The 72-hour incident notification must contain, at minimum, the following elements:

  1. Organisation identification — full legal name, sector classification (essential or important entity), registered address, and the contact details of the designated incident reporting contact (not just a generic mailbox)
  2. Incident description — nature of the incident (e.g., ransomware, DDoS, data breach, insider threat), how and when it was first detected, and by whom
  3. Initial impact assessment — which systems, services, or data are affected; estimated number of users or entities impacted; whether services are currently disrupted
  4. Suspected cause or attack vector — what you know or reasonably believe about how the incident occurred (even if this is preliminary and subject to revision)
  5. Cross-border implications — whether the incident affects or is likely to affect entities, customers, or infrastructure in other EU member states
  6. Immediate containment actions taken — what steps have already been implemented to limit the spread or impact of the incident
  7. Ongoing response measures — what actions are underway and who is leading the response (internal team, external incident response provider, or both)
  8. Whether the incident has been reported to other authorities — including the Data Protection Commission (DPC) if personal data is involved, sector regulators such as the CBI or ComReg, or law enforcement

You will not have perfect answers to all of these at the 72-hour mark. The NIS2 framework accepts that. What it does not accept is silence. Submit what you know, flag what is still being investigated, and commit to providing updates as the picture clarifies.

A Template Structure for Your 72-Hour Notification

The NCSC Ireland has indicated it will provide a formal reporting portal and template. In the interim, the following structure — used by incident response teams across regulated sectors — captures the required elements in a format that regulators expect to see.

📄 72-Hour Incident Notification — Template Structure

TO: NCSC Ireland — incident@ncsc.gov.ie

SUBJECT: NIS2 Incident Notification — [Organisation Name] — [Date of Detection]


1. ORGANISATION DETAILS
Legal name: [Full registered name]
Sector: [e.g., Digital Infrastructure / Financial Market Infrastructure]
Entity classification: [Essential Entity / Important Entity]
Incident contact: [Name, role, direct phone, email]


2. INCIDENT OVERVIEW
Type of incident: [e.g., Ransomware / Data breach / DDoS / Unauthorised access]
Date/time first detected: [DD/MM/YYYY HH:MM]
Detected by: [Internal SOC / External provider / End user / Other]
Current status: [Ongoing / Contained / Resolved]


3. IMPACT ASSESSMENT (PRELIMINARY)
Systems/services affected: [List]
Data affected: [Type and estimated volume — personal data: yes/no]
Estimated users/entities impacted: [Number or range]
Service disruption: [Yes/No — if yes, describe]
Cross-border implications: [Yes/No — if yes, which member states]


4. SUSPECTED CAUSE
[Brief description of suspected attack vector or root cause, clearly labelled as preliminary]


5. CONTAINMENT & RESPONSE ACTIONS TAKEN
[List of immediate actions implemented — e.g., network isolation, credential resets, system shutdowns]


6. ONGOING RESPONSE
Incident response lead: [Name / Organisation]
External support engaged: [Yes/No — if yes, provider name]
Law enforcement notified: [Yes/No — An Garda Síochána / Europol]


7. OTHER REGULATORY NOTIFICATIONS
DPC notified: [Yes/No / Pending assessment]
Sector regulator notified (CBI / ComReg / CRU): [Yes/No / N/A]


8. NEXT STEPS
Expected timeline for full incident report: [Date — within 1 month]
Contact for follow-up queries: [Name, role, contact details]

Print this template. Adapt it to your organisation. Store it in your incident response playbook where it can be found at 2 a.m. on a Sunday — because that is exactly when you may need it.

What Happens If You Miss the 72-Hour Deadline?

Missing the notification deadline is not a procedural inconvenience. It is an independent infringement of S.I. No. 322 of 2024 — with its own penalty exposure, separate from any liability arising from how the underlying incident was managed.

The NCSC Ireland, as the primary competent authority, has a range of enforcement powers available to it. These include:

  • Binding instructions — directing your organisation to take specific remediation actions within a defined timeframe
  • On-site inspections — sending supervisory staff to review your systems, documentation, and processes without extended notice
  • Administrative fines — up to €10 million or 2% of global annual turnover for essential entities; up to €7 million or 1.4% for important entities (whichever is higher)
  • Public disclosure — the NCSC Ireland can publicly identify your organisation as non-compliant, which carries its own reputational and commercial consequences
  • Personal liability referrals — under Regulation 32 of S.I. No. 322 of 2024, the competent authority can hold individual members of the management body personally responsible for infringements

That last point deserves to be read twice. Regulation 32 of S.I. No. 322 of 2024 is not boilerplate. It gives Irish regulators the explicit power to pursue you personally — as a CEO, board chair, or board member — for NIS2 infringements. This is not a liability that sits with the company alone. It sits with the individuals who were responsible for ensuring compliance and failed to do so.

Personal liability under Regulation 32: If the NCSC Ireland or a sector regulator determines that your management body failed to oversee incident reporting obligations — including the 72-hour notification requirement — individual board members and the CEO can be held personally liable. This can include financial penalties at the personal level and, in serious cases, a temporary prohibition on holding management positions. The company's D&O insurance policy may not cover deliberate regulatory non-compliance. Speak to your legal counsel now, not after an incident.

Who in Your Organisation Is Responsible for Notification?

This is a question that must be answered before an incident occurs — not during one. In the fog of an active cyber incident, with systems down, staff panicking, and customers calling, the question of "who actually submits the notification to the NCSC Ireland?" cannot be left unresolved.

Your incident response plan must designate, by name and by role:

  • The incident reporting lead — the person responsible for preparing and submitting regulatory notifications (typically the CISO, Head of IT, or a senior legal/compliance officer)
  • The management sign-off authority — the CEO or a designated board member who must approve the notification before submission
  • The backup contacts — who steps in if the primary reporting lead is unavailable at the time of the incident
  • The external legal counsel — who can provide rapid guidance on whether the incident meets the significance threshold and on any parallel obligations (e.g., GDPR notification to the DPC)

The management sign-off step is particularly important from a personal liability perspective. When a board member approves a regulatory notification, there is a documented record that the management body was engaged, informed, and acting in good faith. That record matters enormously in any subsequent supervisory review.

The Intersection with GDPR: Two Clocks, Not One

If the incident involves personal data — and in most significant cyber incidents, it does — you are running two separate regulatory clocks simultaneously. The GDPR requires notification to the Data Protection Commission (DPC) within 72 hours of becoming aware of a personal data breach. The NIS2 Regulations require notification to the NCSC Ireland within 72 hours of a significant incident.

These are parallel obligations with separate reporting channels, separate content requirements, and separate enforcement regimes. Meeting one does not satisfy the other. Your incident response plan must explicitly address both — including the question of whether the same incident team is handling both notifications, and how you will ensure consistency between the two reports.

The DPC and the NCSC Ireland can and do share information about incidents where both obligations are triggered. An inconsistency between your GDPR breach notification and your NIS2 incident notification is a red flag for both regulators.

The Cost of Getting It Wrong: A Scenario

Scenario — Irish Essential Entity

An Irish-registered technology company, classified as an essential entity under digital infrastructure. 220 employees. EU headquarters for a group with operations in seven member states.

On a Friday afternoon, the security operations function detects unusual outbound traffic suggesting data exfiltration. By Friday evening, the IT team has confirmed that an attacker has had access to internal systems for approximately 72 hours. Customer data — including data from clients in four other EU member states — has likely been accessed.

Senior management decides to engage an external forensic firm before making any regulatory notifications, reasoning that they need to "understand the full picture" first. The forensic firm begins work on Monday morning. The NCSC Ireland is not notified until Wednesday — five days after initial detection, and four days after the significance threshold was clearly met.

The NCSC Ireland initiates a supervisory review. The review finds that the organisation had no documented incident response plan, no designated regulatory reporting lead, and no board-level approval process for incident notifications. The CEO and two board members are identified as personally responsible under Regulation 32.

Outcome: Administrative fine for failure to notify within the required timeframe. A second fine for inadequate incident response procedures under the security requirements of the NIS2 Regulations. Formal personal liability notices issued to the CEO and a board member. Notification shared with competent authorities in four other EU member states, triggering parallel supervisory inquiries. Total estimated cost including legal fees, fines, and remediation: €4–8 million.

The forensic report — the document the management team was waiting for — was delivered three weeks later. It did not prevent a single element of the regulatory consequence.

What Good Looks Like: Your Pre-Incident Checklist

The 72-hour notification deadline is survivable — if your organisation has done the groundwork before an incident occurs. Here is what the NCSC Ireland and sector regulators will expect to see when they review how you handled a significant incident:

  • A documented incident response plan that explicitly covers NIS2 notification obligations, with named roles and escalation paths
  • A pre-completed notification template (like the one above) that your team can populate rapidly under pressure
  • A significance assessment protocol — a written decision framework for determining, quickly and with documentation, whether an incident crosses the NIS2 threshold
  • A designated regulatory reporting contact registered with the NCSC Ireland before any incident occurs
  • A board-level briefing on notification obligations — documented in board minutes, so that management body members can demonstrate they were informed and engaged
  • A tested communication chain — including out-of-hours contact details for the CEO, legal counsel, and the NCSC Ireland reporting channel
  • A parallel GDPR notification protocol — aligned with your NIS2 plan, covering the DPC reporting obligation where personal data is involved
  • A record-keeping system that preserves timestamped evidence of when the incident was detected, when significance was assessed, and when each notification was submitted

None of this needs to be elaborate. A clear, practical, tested plan — approved by the board and stored where your team can find it at any hour — is worth more than a sophisticated framework that nobody has read.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does the 72-hour clock start from the time of the attack, or when we discover it?
It starts from the moment of awareness — when your organisation becomes aware that a significant incident has occurred. This is critically important: if your security monitoring detects an anomaly on Monday and your team confirms it is a significant incident on Tuesday morning, the clock effectively started on Tuesday morning, not on the date the attacker first accessed your systems. Document the exact time and circumstances of your awareness determination. That timestamp will matter.
What if we are not sure whether the incident is "significant"?
When in doubt, notify. The NIS2 framework is designed with a precautionary logic: it is far better to submit a notification that the NCSC Ireland subsequently determines was unnecessary than to conclude the incident was not significant and be found wrong after the fact. A good-faith notification of a borderline incident carries no penalty. A missed notification for an incident that was significant carries significant sanctions. If the picture is genuinely unclear, notify early and flag that your assessment is ongoing.
We are an EU headquarters company registered in Ireland. Do we notify the NCSC Ireland, or the authorities in the countries where we operate?
Your primary reporting obligation is to the NCSC Ireland, as the competent authority in the member state where you are established. The NCSC Ireland is then responsible for sharing information with competent authorities in other affected member states. You should be aware, however, that if your operations in other member states are registered as separate legal entities, those entities may have their own NIS2 reporting obligations to their local competent authorities. This is a common complexity for large multinationals headquartered in Ireland — and it is exactly why your incident response plan needs to address cross-border notification explicitly.
Can we notify NCSC Ireland informally first and follow up with the structured report?
Yes — and this is precisely what the three-stage process is designed to facilitate. The 24-hour early warning is explicitly an informal alert: you are not expected to have a complete picture. Use it to open the channel with the NCSC Ireland and demonstrate that your organisation is responding in real time. Then use the remaining 48 hours to build the structured 72-hour notification. The regulators understand that cyber incidents are chaotic. What they do not accept is silence.
As a board member, what is my personal exposure if the CEO handles the notification process without properly involving me?
This is one of the most important questions any Irish board member can ask. Regulation 32 of S.I. No. 322 of 2024 places personal liability on members of the management body — which includes non-executive directors, not just the CEO. If the NCSC Ireland determines that the management body failed to properly oversee the organisation's NIS2 obligations, individual board members can face personal consequences regardless of whether they were directly involved in the day-to-day incident response. The practical protection is straightforward: ensure the board receives regular briefings on NIS2 compliance status, ensure there is a board-approved incident response plan, and ensure board minutes document this engagement. Your personal liability protection is your documentation trail.
