What Is the NIS2 Directive? A Guide for Irish Companies

Updated 17 April 2026 · Reading time: approx. 8 minutes · Audience: CEOs, Board Chairs, Board Members
Summary
  • NIS2 is already Irish law — transposed as S.I. No. 322 of 2024 in October 2024.
  • It applies to organisations in 18 critical sectors and sets binding cybersecurity obligations for leadership, not just IT teams.
  • Fines reach up to €10 million or 2% of global annual turnover for essential entities.
  • Board members and CEOs face personal liability under Regulation 32 of S.I. 322/2024 — including the possibility of a temporary ban from holding management positions.
  • The NCSC Ireland is your primary competent authority. They are already active.
Key Takeaway

NIS2 is not an IT compliance checkbox. It is a legal obligation that sits squarely on the desks of Irish CEOs and board members — with fines up to €10 million and personal liability for those who fail to act.

There is a new cybersecurity law in Ireland. It came into force in October 2024, and it names you — not your IT manager, not your CTO — as personally responsible for your organisation's cyber resilience. If your organisation falls within scope and you have not yet taken concrete steps to comply, you are already exposed.

This guide is written for CEOs, board chairs, and non-executive directors who need a clear, non-technical explanation of what NIS2 is, why it exists, what it demands from leadership, and what the consequences of inaction look like. You do not need a technical background to read this. You do need to act on it.

Already in force. S.I. No. 322 of 2024 — Ireland's transposition of the NIS2 Directive — has been law since October 2024. The NCSC Ireland (National Cyber Security Centre) is the primary competent authority and is conducting active oversight. There is no grace period remaining.

Why NIS2 Exists — The Problem It Is Solving

The original NIS Directive (2016) was the EU's first attempt at a unified approach to cybersecurity across member states. It was a start — but only that. It covered a narrow set of sectors, set vague standards, and left enforcement largely to individual member states. The result was a patchwork of inconsistent protection across the EU, with significant gaps in critical industries.

Meanwhile, the threat landscape changed dramatically. Ransomware attacks on hospitals paralysed patient care. Attacks on energy infrastructure disrupted entire regions. Supply chain compromises — where attackers entered through a trusted third party — became the norm rather than the exception. The HSE cyberattack in Ireland in 2021, one of the most significant ransomware incidents ever to hit a national health service, was a stark reminder that no organisation is too large, too important, or too well-resourced to be targeted.

NIS2 is the EU's response to that reality. It significantly expands the scope of organisations covered, raises the bar for what counts as adequate cybersecurity, and — critically — places the legal responsibility for cybersecurity at the top of the organisational hierarchy. This is not a technical upgrade to an IT framework. It is a governance overhaul, and it starts with the board.

What Is NIS2 in Plain English?

The NIS2 Directive (EU 2022/2555) is an EU-wide law that requires organisations operating in critical and important sectors to implement robust cybersecurity measures, report serious incidents quickly, and ensure that their leadership takes active responsibility for cyber risk management.

In Ireland, NIS2 has been transposed into national law as S.I. No. 322 of 2024. This statutory instrument is the binding legal instrument. It is not aspirational guidance. It is law, and breaches carry significant penalties.

The directive operates on a simple logic: the organisations that underpin modern society — energy providers, hospitals, banks, transport networks, water utilities, digital infrastructure providers — carry systemic risk. If they are disrupted by a cyberattack, the consequences extend far beyond the organisation itself. Society depends on them functioning. Therefore, they must be held to a higher standard of cyber resilience, and their leadership must be accountable for achieving it.

Does NIS2 Apply to Your Organisation?

NIS2 applies to medium and large organisations operating in one of 18 designated sectors. Two thresholds determine whether you fall within scope:

  • Essential Entities: Organisations with 250 or more employees, or annual turnover exceeding €50 million, in high-criticality sectors.
  • Important Entities: Organisations with 50 or more employees, or annual turnover exceeding €10 million, in sectors of significant importance.

The 18 sectors covered include energy, transport, banking, financial market infrastructure, health, drinking water, wastewater, digital infrastructure, ICT service management, public administration, space, postal and courier services, waste management, chemicals, food production and distribution, manufacturing of critical products, digital providers (including online marketplaces, search engines, and social networks), and research.

For Irish-registered companies, there is an additional complexity worth noting. Ireland hosts the European headquarters of a significant number of large multinational technology companies, pharmaceutical groups, and financial services firms. If your organisation is incorporated or has its principal EU establishment in Ireland, you are likely subject to Irish NIS2 supervision — regardless of where the majority of your operations are based. This makes cross-border compliance obligations particularly relevant for Irish-registered essential entities.

Not sure if you're in scope? The safest assumption for any mid-sized or large organisation operating in one of the 18 sectors is that NIS2 applies. The cost of being wrong — in either direction — is asymmetric. Missing your obligations is far more expensive than confirming them. The NCSC Ireland (ncsc.gov.ie) is the primary competent authority for most sectors; sector-specific regulators include the Central Bank of Ireland (CBI) for financial services, ComReg for telecoms and digital infrastructure, and the CRU for energy.

What NIS2 Actually Requires — The Four Core Obligations

For a non-technical reader, the obligations under NIS2 can be understood through four broad categories. Each one has direct implications for board governance, not just operational IT management.

1. Risk Management and Security Measures

NIS2 requires organisations to implement appropriate and proportionate technical and organisational measures to manage cybersecurity risk. This is not a vague aspiration — it covers ten specific domains: risk analysis and information system security policies, incident handling, business continuity and crisis management, supply chain security, security in network and information systems acquisition, vulnerability disclosure and handling, cybersecurity hygiene and training, cryptography and encryption, human resources security and access control, and the use of multi-factor authentication.

Critically, it is not enough to have technical measures in place. The board must formally approve the organisation's security policies, and that approval must be documented. Leadership is expected to understand the measures they are approving — not at a technical level, but at a governance level. Ignorance is not a defence.

2. Incident Reporting

When a significant cybersecurity incident occurs, the law requires your organisation to act quickly and transparently. The reporting timeline under S.I. No. 322 of 2024 is as follows:

  • Within 24 hours: Submit an early warning to the NCSC Ireland indicating that a significant incident has occurred.
  • Within 72 hours: Submit a full incident notification, including an initial assessment of the incident's severity and impact.
  • Within one month: Submit a final report providing a comprehensive account of the incident, its root cause, and the measures taken.

These are not aspirational timelines. They are legal deadlines. Organisations that experience an incident and attempt to manage it quietly — without notifying the NCSC Ireland — face compounded penalties: one for the security failure and a separate one for failing to report.

3. Supply Chain Security

NIS2 explicitly extends the compliance obligation beyond your own organisation. You are required to assess and manage the cybersecurity risks posed by your suppliers and service providers. If a critical vendor has poor security practices and that becomes a vector for an attack on your systems, you cannot simply point at the vendor. The law expects you to have evaluated their security posture before and during your relationship with them.

For many Irish organisations — particularly in financial services, pharma, and technology — this represents a significant shift. Supply chain security reviews must be part of procurement processes, not an afterthought.

4. Board Governance and Personal Accountability

This is the obligation that most CEOs and board members have not yet fully internalised. Regulation 32 of S.I. 322/2024 makes members of the management body personally liable for infringements of the NIS2 obligations. This is not a theoretical provision. It means that if your organisation fails to comply, the competent authority can take enforcement action directly against individual directors and executives — not just against the organisation as a legal entity.

The potential consequences for individuals include formal censure, public reprimands, and — in serious cases — a temporary prohibition on exercising management functions. That is not a fine paid from the corporate balance sheet. That is a personal consequence that follows you individually.

The Penalties — In Concrete Terms

The financial penalties under Irish NIS2 law are structured as follows:

Category Maximum Fine Alternative Cap
Essential Entity €10 million 2% of global annual turnover
Important Entity €7 million 1.4% of global annual turnover

Whichever is higher applies. For a large multinational with its European headquarters in Ireland and €5 billion in global turnover, the 2% cap translates to a potential fine of €100 million — far exceeding the headline €10 million figure. Ireland's status as a hub for large multinationals makes this calculation particularly significant.

It is also worth understanding that fines can stack. A single incident can generate multiple breaches — failure to have adequate security measures, failure to report within 24 hours, failure to submit a complete 72-hour notification. Each is a separate infringement, and each can attract a separate penalty.

Personal Liability — What It Means for You Specifically

The personal liability provisions of NIS2 represent the most significant governance shift the directive introduces. Under Regulation 32 of S.I. 322/2024, members of the management body — which includes executive directors, non-executive directors, and board chairs — can be held personally accountable for the organisation's compliance failures.

What does this look like in practice? The NCSC Ireland or a relevant sector regulator can:

  • Issue a formal public statement identifying you by name as responsible for a compliance failure
  • Require you to attend and account for the organisation's security posture
  • Recommend or impose a temporary ban on your ability to hold management functions within the sector

The key point is that these consequences cannot be absorbed by the company. They attach to you personally. No indemnity from the company, no D&O insurance policy, no board resolution adopted after the fact will retroactively satisfy the requirement that you — as a member of the management body — actively approved, oversaw, and documented the organisation's cybersecurity measures while they were being implemented.

The documentation requirement is central. NIS2 expects boards to maintain a clear audit trail showing that cybersecurity was treated as a governance matter: board minutes reflecting cybersecurity discussions, formal approval of security policies, records of board-level training on cyber risks. If you cannot produce that documentation, the presumption will work against you.

Illustrative Scenario

Scenario: Irish-registered mid-size manufacturing company, 310 employees, annual turnover €45 million.

Classified as an essential entity under NIS2. In early 2026, the company is hit by a ransomware attack that encrypts production systems and causes a week of operational downtime. The CEO is alerted on day one. The board is briefed informally. The decision is made to engage an external incident response firm and attempt to resolve the situation before making any external notifications, on the basis that "we don't want to alarm customers or regulators while we're still assessing the situation."

The 24-hour early warning deadline passes. The 72-hour notification deadline passes. Seven days after the initial incident, a major client — whose supply chain was disrupted — files a complaint with the NCSC Ireland.

NCSC Ireland initiates a supervisory review. During that review, it emerges that the company had no formally adopted incident response plan, no documented board approval of security policies, and no record of board-level cybersecurity training. The CEO and two board members are identified under Regulation 32 as personally responsible for the governance failures.

Outcome: Administrative fine for failure to report within the statutory deadlines. A second fine for inadequate security measures under the risk management obligations. Formal censure of the CEO and a named non-executive director. Total estimated cost — including fines, legal representation, incident response, and remediation — in excess of €3–5 million. Reputational damage to both the company and the named individuals is ongoing.

None of this was the result of an unusually sophisticated attack. It was the result of an organisation that was not prepared — and a board that had not treated cybersecurity as a governance matter.

Common Questions from Irish Boards

We haven't had a cyber incident. Does NIS2 still apply to us?
Yes. NIS2 compliance is not conditional on having experienced an attack. The obligation to have documented security measures in place, to have a board-approved security policy, and to maintain incident reporting procedures exists regardless of your incident history. A supervisory review by the NCSC Ireland can be triggered at any time — by a complaint, a sector-wide audit, or a proactive inspection. Your current absence of incidents does not reduce your compliance obligation by one iota.
Can we simply delegate NIS2 compliance to our IT team or CISO?
You can delegate the operational implementation — but you cannot delegate the legal accountability. Regulation 32 of S.I. 322/2024 is explicit: the management body is personally liable. That means the CEO and board members must actively approve the organisation's security measures, oversee their implementation, and be able to demonstrate they have done so. Your CISO can run the programme. You cannot hand them the liability.
We are an Irish-registered subsidiary of a US parent company. Which rules apply?
If your principal EU establishment is in Ireland, Irish NIS2 law applies — meaning NCSC Ireland is your competent authority for most obligations. Depending on your sector, CBI, ComReg, or CRU may also have supervisory roles. The fact that your parent company operates under US cybersecurity frameworks does not substitute for Irish NIS2 compliance. You will need to ensure your Irish entity meets the obligations independently, and you should document that clearly for both the NCSC and your parent.
What is the first thing we should do as a board?
Establish where you actually stand. That means commissioning a structured gap analysis that maps your current security posture against the ten obligation domains in NIS2, identifies what documentation is missing, and produces a prioritised action plan. This is not a multi-year programme — a focused gap analysis can be completed in a matter of weeks. The output gives your board a defensible position: you knew the requirements, you assessed your position, and you put a plan in place. That matters enormously if a supervisory review is ever initiated.
We are already ISO 27001 certified. Does that mean we are NIS2 compliant?
ISO 27001 is a strong foundation and significantly overlaps with NIS2 obligations — particularly in risk management, access control, and security documentation. However, NIS2 introduces specific requirements around incident reporting timelines, supply chain security, and board governance accountability that are not fully addressed by ISO 27001 alone. Certification helps, but it does not automatically satisfy the legal requirements of S.I. No. 322 of 2024. A targeted NIS2 gap analysis will identify precisely where your ISO framework is sufficient and where additional steps are needed.

The Timeline You Need to Understand

There is no future start date to wait for. S.I. No. 322 of 2024 is in force. The NCSC Ireland is the competent authority and is conducting supervisory activities now. Sector regulators including the CBI and ComReg are also active within their respective domains.

The organisations that are best placed are those that treat NIS2 as a governance programme — not an IT project — and that have started now. Every month without documented security policies, without a board-approved incident response plan, and without a formal supply chain security review is a month of unmitigated legal exposure for you personally and for your organisation.

The good news — and there is good news — is that getting to a defensible position is achievable. Most organisations in scope already have many of the underlying security controls in place. What they typically lack is the documentation, the governance structure, and the board-level approvals that convert those technical controls into NIS2 compliance. That gap can be closed methodically, quickly, and without disrupting operations.

The first step is understanding exactly where you stand. Everything else follows from that.
