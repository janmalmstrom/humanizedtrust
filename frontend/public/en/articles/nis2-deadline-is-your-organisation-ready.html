NIS2 Is Already in Force: Is Your Organisation Ready?

Updated 19 April 2026 · Read time: 8–10 minutes · For: CEOs, Board Chairs, Directors
Summary
  • NIS2 came into force in Ireland in October 2024 (S.I. No. 322 of 2024).
  • Fines up to €10 million or 2% of global turnover for essential entities; up to €7 million or 1.4% for important entities.
  • Board members are personally liable for compliance failures — not just the organisation.
  • NCSC Ireland, CBI, ComReg, and CRU are already conducting inspections. Waiting is no longer an option.
  • Three company profiles: fully ready, partially ready, not started. Yours is probably one of them.
Critical Insight

NIS2 is not an IT problem — it is personal liability for you as a CEO or board member, with fines up to €10 million and potential professional disqualification if your organisation falls short.

The NIS2 Directive became Irish law in October 2024. If you have not yet verified whether your organisation is covered, you have already lost three months of preparation time.

The consequences of delay are not abstract. They are:

  • Fines up to €10 million for essential entities (or 2% of global turnover — whichever is higher)
  • Personal liability for board members under Regulation 32 of S.I. 322/2024
  • Potential professional disqualification — a ban on holding director or senior management roles
  • Mandatory public disclosure of your organisation's compliance status if inspected
  • Incident response timelines of 24 hours (early warning) to 72 hours (full notification)
NCSC Ireland, along with sector regulators (CBI, ComReg, CRU), has already begun inspections. The window for quiet preparation is closed. If your organisation is essential or important and you have not documented your compliance position, you are exposed.

What NIS2 Means in Ireland

Ireland transposed the NIS2 Directive into national law via S.I. No. 322 of 2024 (the NIS2 Regulations). This places 18 economic sectors under mandatory cybersecurity and incident reporting requirements.

Two categories of organisations are in scope:

  • Essential entities: organisations with systemically critical roles (typically more than 250 staff or €50 million+ annual turnover)
  • Important entities: significant organisations in regulated sectors (50–249 staff or €10–50 million turnover)

The 18 sectors include: energy, transport, banking, financial market infrastructure, health, drinking water, wastewater, digital infrastructure, ICT service providers, public administration, space, postal services, waste management, chemicals, food production, manufacturing, digital service providers, and research organisations.

Many Irish-registered organisations fall into this net. If your company has a major Irish presence — particularly if it is a European headquarters for a larger group — the likelihood is high.

Irish context: Large tech, pharmaceutical, and financial services groups headquartered in Ireland are automatically in scope. Even if your operations are global, Irish registration triggers Irish NIS2 compliance obligations.

The Penalty Structure — and It Is Real

Penalties are designed to hurt. They scale by entity category and can compound across multiple breaches.

Entity Category Maximum Fine Alternative (if higher)
Essential Entity €10 million 2% of global annual turnover
Important Entity €7 million 1.4% of global annual turnover

The fine is whichever is highest. A company with €500 million in global revenue classified as essential faces a potential €10 million fine. A company with €600 million classified as important faces a potential €8.4 million fine (1.4% of turnover).

These are not per-organisation fines. They are per breach, per incident, and per failure to comply with a specific control requirement. A single inspection can result in multiple, stacked penalties.

Personal Liability — Your Name Is on the Hook

Regulation 32 of S.I. 322/2024 contains language that will be familiar to those who have read GDPR guidance on data protection officer responsibilities. It states that members of the management body (board members, executive directors, and often senior managers) are personally liable for compliance failures.

This means:

  • You cannot hide behind the company's legal entity. The fine can be issued against you personally.
  • You cannot claim ignorance. Once you are aware of NIS2, you bear a duty of care to understand and oversee compliance.
  • You cannot delegate your way out. NCSC Ireland and sector regulators expect board-level awareness and sign-off of security measures.
  • You face potential disqualification from holding director or senior officer roles if cited for a serious breach.

In practice, this means your board papers must document:

  • Board approval of your cybersecurity policy
  • Board awareness of NIS2-required controls
  • Evidence that the board regularly reviews compliance status
  • Board sign-off on incident response procedures

If your board has never discussed NIS2 on record, you are already in a precarious position.

Incident Reporting Timelines — 24 Hours Is Not a Draft

NIS2 requires organisations to notify NCSC Ireland of serious cybersecurity incidents within strict timelines:

  • 24 hours: Early warning (preliminary notification)
  • 72 hours: Incident notification (initial assessment submitted)
  • 1 month: Final incident report (full analysis, root cause, remediation)

These are hard deadlines. Missing them is itself a breach, triggering separate sanctions. Organisations that experience an incident and try to "investigate quietly" before reporting will face both an incident penalty and a reporting violation penalty.

Common mistake: Assuming you have time to complete forensics before notifying. You don't. NCSC Ireland expects initial contact within 24 hours, with best-effort information supplied at 72 hours. Completeness comes later, but silence does not.

Three Company Profiles: Where Does Yours Sit?

Profile 1: Fully Ready

The organisation: A financial services firm with 300 employees, essential entity status, €80 million in revenue. Board recognises NIS2 as a strategic priority.

What they have done:

  • Completed a formal gap analysis against all ten NIS2 control domains
  • Board has approved a three-year cybersecurity roadmap, documented in meeting minutes
  • Incident response plan written, roles assigned, tested in a tabletop exercise (documented)
  • All staff (including executives) trained on NIS2 requirements and incident reporting
  • Risk management framework in place, covering supply chain and third-party security
  • CTO reports to the board quarterly on compliance status using a scoreboard KPI (0–100 scale)
  • NCSC Ireland has already been contacted proactively; relationship is established

Their risk profile: Low to minimal. If inspected, they have evidence of governance, policy, and execution. They are not free from risk — no organisation is — but they have built a defensible position.

Cost to reach this state: €250,000–€400,000 over 18 months (external consultancy, training, tooling, staff time). Cost if fined: €10 million. The math is simple.

Profile 2: Partially Ready

The organisation: A healthcare provider with 180 employees, important entity status, €45 million revenue. IT is engaged; board is vaguely aware.

What they have done:

  • IT team has drafted a security policy, but it has never been formally approved by the board
  • Incident response procedures exist on paper, but there has been no test or drill
  • Some staff have been trained; senior management has not
  • Risk assessment was completed 18 months ago; no recent refresh
  • Supply chain security is acknowledged as "something we need to do"
  • Board minutes do not reference NIS2 explicitly; it is treated as an IT issue, not a governance issue

Their risk profile: Moderate to high. They have done some work, but the foundation is shaky. If NCSC Ireland requests documentation of board governance, they will be unable to provide it. If an incident occurs, their response plan is untested and cannot be executed quickly.

What will happen if inspected: Regulators will note that security is not a board-level concern. This alone is a red flag. Missing board documentation of policy approval will lead to findings. Multiple deficiencies — some governance, some procedural — will compound into a fine of €3–6 million and mandatory remediation orders.

Time to full readiness: 9–12 months. Cost: €150,000–€250,000 plus executive time.

Profile 3: Not Started

The organisation: A transport company with 120 employees, important entity status, €32 million revenue. NIS2 is on a to-do list, but no one owns it.

What they have done:

  • Heard of NIS2 in passing; unsure if they are in scope
  • No formal security policy; security is handled ad-hoc by IT
  • No documented incident response plan
  • No training beyond standard password awareness
  • No regular risk assessments
  • Board has never discussed NIS2
  • No contact with NCSC Ireland

Their risk profile: Critical. If inspected — which could happen as the result of a breach, a complaint, or routine sector sampling — they will fail every audit point.

What will happen if inspected: NCSC Ireland will find no evidence of board governance, no formal policies, no documented procedures, no training records, and no incident response readiness. The organisation will be issued with a compliance notice and given 90 days to remediate. Simultaneously, a fine of €5–7 million will be issued for systemic non-compliance. The CEO and board will be notified of personal liability findings. The company's sector regulator (in this case, transport authorities) will be looped in.

Time to minimum readiness: 6–9 months, but under duress and with reputational damage already done. Cost: €200,000–€300,000 in crisis remediation, plus legal fees, plus fine.

What Inspection Actually Looks Like

NCSC Ireland does not phone in advance. Inspection typically begins with a written request for documentation:

  • Board minutes and decisions related to cybersecurity policy
  • Risk assessments and management frameworks
  • Incident response procedures and test records
  • Staff training logs
  • Supply chain security agreements
  • Evidence of control implementation (access logs, encryption inventories, backup test results)

The timeline is typically 14–30 days to respond. Organisations that do not have this documentation immediately available are in trouble. If you have it in email chains and spreadsheets scattered across team inboxes, you are not ready to answer.

If the initial document review raises concerns, NCSC Ireland will follow up with site visits, staff interviews, and technical audits. This process can take weeks or months.

Real-World Scenario

Financial Services Firm, 280 Staff, €65M Revenue — Essential Entity

In February 2026, the firm experiences a credential compromise affecting customer data access. The incident is contained within 36 hours, but critical customer files may have been exfiltrated.

The organisation fails to notify NCSC Ireland within 24 hours — instead, it notifies its legal team and takes 5 days to decide whether disclosure is necessary. By the time notification occurs, the 24-hour early warning deadline has passed.

NCSC Ireland initiates an investigation. During the inspection, regulators discover that:

  • The incident response plan exists but has never been tested
  • The board has never formally approved the cybersecurity policy
  • Risk assessments are over 24 months old
  • The CTO reports to the CFO, not the board

Outcome: Fine of €8 million for the incident plus failure to report on time. Additional fine of €1.5 million for systemic control gaps (policy, risk management, governance). Total: €9.5 million. The CEO is named in the findings as personally liable for governance failure. The board's insurance does not cover regulatory penalties.

Preventable cost: A €200,000 gap analysis and 6 months of documented remediation would have avoided the entire penalty and preserved the organisation's reputation.
