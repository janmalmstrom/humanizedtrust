Technical Compliance & Article 21

NIS2 Technical Security Requirements — What the Law Actually Demands

Updated 17 April 2026 · Reading time: approx. 8 minutes · Audience: CEOs, Board Members
Summary
  • Article 21 of NIS2 — transposed into Irish law via S.I. No. 322 of 2024 — sets out specific technical security measures your organisation must implement and document.
  • Six areas demand your immediate attention: MFA, encryption, backups, patch management, access control, and logging.
  • NCSC Ireland can inspect these measures at any time — without a prior incident occurring.
  • Under Regulation 32 of S.I. 322/2024, you as CEO or board member are personally liable if these measures are absent or inadequate.
  • Fines for essential entities reach up to €10 million or 2% of global turnover.
Key Takeaway

Article 21 is not a wish list for your IT team. It is a legal checklist that your board must approve, monitor, and document. If it is missing when NCSC Ireland comes knocking, the personal consequences fall on you — not your systems administrator.

Most CEOs think about NIS2 in terms of fines and reporting deadlines. That is understandable — the numbers are alarming enough to grab attention. But there is a quieter, more immediate risk sitting inside Article 21 of the Directive: the technical security measures your organisation is legally required to have in place right now.

These are not aspirational guidelines. Under S.I. No. 322 of 2024 — Ireland's transposition of NIS2, which came into force in October 2024 — organisations in scope must be able to demonstrate, at any point, that specific technical controls are operational and documented. NCSC Ireland does not need to wait for a breach before it inspects you. A single complaint from a business partner or a routine supervisory review is enough to trigger scrutiny.

This article breaks down the six core technical requirements in plain language. Not for your IT team — for you. Because under Regulation 32 of S.I. 322/2024, it is your name on the liability, not your Head of IT's.

Personal liability is real and immediate. Regulation 32 of S.I. 322/2024 allows NCSC Ireland to hold individual members of the management body personally liable for infringements. That includes the CEO, the board chair, and board directors. Delegating technical decisions to IT does not transfer that liability away from you.

What Article 21 Actually Says

Article 21 of the NIS2 Directive — and by extension the Irish Regulations — requires organisations to take "appropriate and proportionate technical, operational and organisational measures" to manage cybersecurity risks. The law does not leave this vague. It specifies a list of mandatory domains that every essential and important entity must address.

The ten domains include: risk analysis and information system security policies; incident handling; business continuity, backup management and disaster recovery; supply chain security; security in network and information systems acquisition, development and maintenance; policies and procedures to assess the effectiveness of cybersecurity measures; basic cyber hygiene practices and cybersecurity training; policies and procedures regarding the use of cryptography and encryption; human resources security, access control policies and asset management; and the use of multi-factor authentication and secure communication systems.

Six of these domains — MFA, encryption, backups, patch management, access control, and logging — are where most Irish organisations have identifiable gaps. These are the areas NCSC Ireland is most likely to probe first, and they are the areas where the distance between "we think we're compliant" and "we can prove we're compliant" is largest.

1. Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

Article 21 explicitly names multi-factor authentication as a required security measure. This is not optional for in-scope organisations. MFA must be enforced — not merely available — across all systems that touch critical services, administrative access, remote connections, and email.

The question to ask your IT team is not "do we have MFA?" It is: "Where is MFA not enforced, and why not?" Most organisations have MFA switched on for some systems and quietly exempted from others — legacy applications, shared service accounts, third-party integrations. Every exemption is a potential liability.

NCSC Ireland will expect to see a documented MFA policy, evidence of deployment across critical access points, and a process for managing exceptions. If your IT team cannot produce these within 48 hours, you have a gap.

What to ask your IT team:

  • Is MFA enforced on all admin accounts, email accounts, and remote access without exception?
  • Do we have a written policy that defines where MFA is required and how exceptions are approved?
  • Are we using phishing-resistant MFA methods (hardware keys or device-bound authentication) for privileged access?

2. Encryption

The NIS2 Regulations require the use of cryptography and encryption as part of protecting network and information systems. In practical terms, this means data at rest and data in transit must be encrypted — and you need to be able to prove it.

Many organisations encrypt data in transit (HTTPS, TLS) but have significant gaps in encryption at rest — particularly for archived data, backup files, removable media, and data held in cloud environments managed by third parties. Under NIS2, your supplier's encryption posture is also your responsibility. If a managed service provider holds your data unencrypted, the liability does not sit with them alone.

There is also a key management dimension. Encrypting data without managing encryption keys properly is a false sense of security. Article 21 compliance requires not just encryption, but documented policies covering how encryption keys are generated, stored, rotated, and revoked.

What to ask your IT team:

  • Is all sensitive data encrypted at rest and in transit, and do we have documentation to prove it?
  • Do we have a key management policy and process, or are encryption keys stored alongside the data they protect?
  • Have we verified our cloud and managed service providers' encryption standards contractually?

3. Backups and Business Continuity

Article 21 requires business continuity planning, backup management, and disaster recovery capabilities. For many organisations, this is the area where the gap between intention and documented reality is widest.

Having backups is not the same as having a NIS2-compliant backup regime. The Regulations require that backups are: taken at a defined frequency; stored separately from production systems (including offline or immutable copies that cannot be encrypted by ransomware); tested regularly; and recoverable within a documented recovery time objective.

The word "tested" is important. A backup that has never been restored is not a backup — it is a file. NCSC Ireland will look for evidence of regular recovery testing, not just evidence that backup software is running.

"A backup that has never been tested is not a recovery asset. It is an untested assumption."

What to ask your IT team:

  • When was our last full backup restoration test, and is it documented?
  • Do we hold immutable or air-gapped backup copies that cannot be modified or deleted by ransomware?
  • Do we have a documented recovery time objective (RTO) and recovery point objective (RPO) approved by the board?

4. Patch Management

Unpatched systems are the single most common entry point for cyberattacks on European critical infrastructure. Article 21's requirement to maintain the security of network and information systems includes a clear obligation to manage vulnerabilities through timely patching and system updates.

A compliant patch management programme requires: a current asset inventory (you cannot patch what you have not catalogued); a defined patching cadence with escalation timelines for critical vulnerabilities; documented exceptions with a compensating control and a review date; and evidence that end-of-life systems have been identified and are being managed.

Many Irish organisations — particularly those running complex or legacy environments — have systems that have not been patched in months or years. Under NIS2, this is not an IT problem to be solved at the next budget cycle. It is a current legal exposure.

Ireland context: Many large organisations with their EU headquarters in Ireland — including major technology companies and financial services firms — operate complex hybrid environments with significant legacy infrastructure. NCSC Ireland is well aware of this reality. That awareness does not translate into tolerance; it translates into more targeted inspection.

What to ask your IT team:

  • Do we have a complete, up-to-date inventory of all systems, including cloud assets and end-of-life hardware?
  • What is our documented patching policy, and what are the timelines for critical, high, and medium severity patches?
  • Are there any systems currently running beyond their vendor support lifecycle, and what compensating controls are in place?

5. Access Control

Access control under Article 21 means ensuring that the right people have access to the right systems — and, crucially, that no one has access they do not need. This principle, known as least privilege, is foundational to NIS2 compliance and one of the most commonly violated in practice.

The specific areas NCSC Ireland will examine include: privileged access management (are administrative credentials restricted and monitored?); joiners, movers and leavers processes (when a staff member changes role or leaves, are their access rights updated or revoked promptly?); third-party and supplier access (are external parties given time-limited, scoped access or standing access to your systems?); and service account governance (are system-to-system accounts inventoried and reviewed?).

The access control picture in most organisations is significantly messier than senior leadership realises. Former employees with active credentials, contractors with unchanged permissions from years ago, and shared admin passwords are common findings in any gap analysis. Under NIS2, each of these is a documented deficiency.

What to ask your IT team:

  • Do we have a formal access review process, and when was it last completed?
  • Can we produce a current list of all users with privileged or administrative access and confirm each one is justified?
  • How do we manage access for suppliers and third parties — do they have time-limited, scoped access, or standing access to production systems?

6. Logging and Audit Trails

You cannot detect what you have not recorded. Article 21 requires organisations to maintain the capability to monitor, detect, and respond to security incidents — and that depends entirely on having comprehensive, tamper-evident logging in place.

A compliant logging programme covers: system and application event logs; authentication and access logs (who logged in, from where, when); administrative activity logs (what changes were made to systems and by whom); and network traffic logs sufficient to reconstruct the timeline of a security incident. Logs must be retained for a defined period — typically a minimum of twelve months under NIS2 guidance — and stored in a location separate from the systems they monitor, so that an attacker who compromises a system cannot also erase the evidence.

The practical compliance test here is simple: if NCSC Ireland notified you of a security incident that occurred six months ago, could you reconstruct what happened? If the answer is uncertain, your logging posture is not compliant.

There is also a direct connection to the incident reporting obligation under S.I. 322/2024. The 24-hour early warning and 72-hour notification deadlines to NCSC Ireland are only achievable if you have logging infrastructure that allows rapid detection and scoping of incidents. Organisations without adequate logging are not just non-compliant on Article 21 — they are structurally incapable of meeting the reporting timelines in Article 23.

What to ask your IT team:

  • What logs do we currently collect, how long are they retained, and are they stored separately from production systems?
  • Do we have a security monitoring capability — whether in-house or via a managed service — that reviews logs for suspicious activity?
  • Could we reconstruct the timeline of a breach that occurred three months ago using our current log data?

The Documentation Requirement: Why Evidence Matters More Than Intent

Here is the aspect of Article 21 that most CEOs do not fully grasp until it is too late: NCSC Ireland does not assess what you intend to do, or what you believe you are doing. It assesses what you can prove.

Every one of the six technical areas above requires not just implementation, but documented evidence of implementation. A security measure that exists but is not documented is, from a regulatory standpoint, a security measure that cannot be verified — and an unverified measure is treated as absent.

The documentation NCSC Ireland expects to see includes:

  • Written policies for each security domain, approved and signed off by the board
  • Evidence of implementation — configuration records, deployment logs, testing reports
  • Review records showing that controls are regularly assessed and updated
  • Board minutes or resolutions demonstrating management oversight of cybersecurity measures, as required by Regulation 32 of S.I. 322/2024

This last point is critical. Regulation 32 requires the management body — not just IT — to approve cybersecurity measures, oversee their implementation, and be accountable for infringements. The board must be demonstrably engaged. A board resolution from two years ago that broadly endorses "an IT security policy" is not sufficient. Active, informed oversight is required, and it must be evidenced in board records.

The gap between "we have it" and "we can prove it" is where fines are generated. Under S.I. 322/2024, NCSC Ireland can impose fines of up to €10 million or 2% of global annual turnover on essential entities — and up to €7 million or 1.4% on important entities — for failure to implement adequate technical measures. These sanctions apply whether or not a breach has occurred.

A Practical Scenario: What an NCSC Ireland Inspection Looks Like

Illustrative Scenario

Scenario: Irish-registered essential entity in the digital infrastructure sector, approximately 300 employees, EU headquarters in Dublin.

The organisation has a capable IT team and genuinely believes its security posture is strong. There has been no significant incident. In early 2026, NCSC Ireland initiates a supervisory review following a routine sector-wide inspection programme.

NCSC Ireland requests documentation within 21 days. The IT team can demonstrate working MFA and encryption for primary systems. However, the review reveals: no written MFA policy covering exceptions; backup restoration tests not completed in over fourteen months and not documented; a patch management process that exists in practice but has no formal written policy; access reviews last completed eighteen months ago with no record of former employee credential revocation; and logging retention of only 45 days — well below the 12-month standard expected under NIS2 guidance.

Critically, there is no board-level documentation demonstrating that management approved or oversaw these security measures, as required by Regulation 32. The organisation can demonstrate competent IT practice but cannot demonstrate governance.

Outcome: NCSC Ireland issues a binding instruction requiring remediation within 60 days. A supervisory fine is issued for failure to implement adequate technical measures and failure of management oversight. The CEO is formally notified of personal liability under Regulation 32. Total estimated cost — including fine, external legal support, emergency remediation, and reputational management — €800,000 to €1.4 million. None of this was caused by a cyberattack. It was caused by a documentation gap.

The Irish Context: Why This Matters More Here

Ireland occupies a unique position in the NIS2 landscape. As the EU headquarters for a large number of major technology companies, pharmaceutical firms, and financial services organisations, Irish-registered essential entities often have operations, systems, and supply chains that span multiple EU member states.

Under NIS2's cross-border provisions, an Irish-registered entity that experiences a significant incident may be subject to coordinated review involving not just NCSC Ireland but also sector regulators such as the Central Bank of Ireland (CBI) for financial services or ComReg for telecoms and digital infrastructure. The CRU oversees the energy sector. Each of these regulators has independent enforcement powers, meaning a single compliance failure can attract parallel investigations.

For organisations with operations in multiple EU countries, the Irish registration does not insulate you from obligations in other jurisdictions — it makes you the primary point of accountability for the entire EU footprint. The technical measures described in Article 21 must be implemented and evidenced at an enterprise level, not just in the Irish subsidiary.

Questions CEOs Should Bring to the Next Board Meeting

You do not need to become a cybersecurity expert. You need to ask the right questions — and ensure the answers are documented. Here are six questions that map directly to Article 21 and Regulation 32:

  1. Can our IT team produce written policies for MFA, encryption, backup, patching, access control, and logging within 48 hours? If not, those policies do not exist in a form that satisfies NCSC Ireland.
  2. Has the board formally approved our cybersecurity measures? Is that approval recorded in board minutes with enough specificity to demonstrate active oversight?
  3. When did we last test our backup recovery, and what was the result? The date and documented outcome should be immediately available.
  4. What is our current patch status for critical systems? Your IT team should be able to tell you the age of the oldest unpatched critical vulnerability in your environment.
  5. Who currently has privileged or administrative access to our systems, and is every one of those accounts still justified?
  6. If NCSC Ireland notified us of a breach that occurred 90 days ago, could we reconstruct what happened using our logs?

If any of these questions produce uncertain or slow answers, you have identified a compliance gap. The good news is that every one of these gaps can be closed with structured effort over a defined period. The risk is in leaving them open.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do these technical requirements apply even if we haven't had a cyber incident?
Yes. Article 21 compliance is a continuous legal obligation, not a response to an incident. NCSC Ireland can inspect your technical measures at any time as part of routine supervisory activity. The absence of a prior incident does not reduce your obligation or your liability.
What does "proportionate" mean in practice — are smaller organisations held to the same standard?
The law uses the word "proportionate" to reflect that a 60-person important entity and a 3,000-person essential entity will implement these measures differently in terms of technology and cost. But the obligation itself applies equally. A smaller organisation cannot claim exemption from MFA or logging on the grounds of size — it can implement these measures in a way that is scaled to its resources. What it cannot do is omit them.
We use cloud services for most of our infrastructure. Are we still responsible for these technical measures?
Yes. NIS2 and S.I. 322/2024 place the compliance obligation on your organisation, regardless of where your systems are hosted. If a cloud provider manages your backups, encryption, or access controls, you are responsible for ensuring those services meet NIS2 standards — and for documenting that assurance contractually. Supply chain security is itself a named Article 21 domain. Your provider's compliance posture is your responsibility to verify.
How quickly can we become compliant if we identify gaps today?
Many organisations can close the documentation gaps within 60 to 90 days with structured external support. Some technical gaps — particularly around logging infrastructure or backup architecture — may take longer to fully remediate. The key is to start with a gap analysis that gives you a prioritised picture of your current position, then work through remediation in order of risk and regulatory exposure. The worst position to be in is starting that process after NCSC Ireland has already been in touch.
What is the personal liability risk if we are found non-compliant with Article 21?
Under Regulation 32 of S.I. 322/2024, management body members — including the CEO and board directors — can be held personally liable for infringements of the NIS2 Regulations. In serious cases, NCSC Ireland can issue an order temporarily prohibiting an individual from exercising managerial responsibilities. This is not a fine against the company. It is a professional prohibition against you personally. It cannot be reversed by a retrospective board resolution.
