Your personal liability under NIS2

NIS2 (Ireland's full transposition is expected in 2026 — board liability provisions will apply) makes it clear: you as a board member or CEO will be personally liable for ensuring that cybersecurity measures are approved, monitored and implemented. The supervisory authority can, for repeated violations, prohibit you from holding a management position — not just fine the company. Ignorance is no defence: the law requires you to undergo specific cybersecurity training.

Art. 20–21 NIS2 (Irish transposition expected 2026) 1 — Governance documentation — what must exist
Document What it must contain Done?
Cybersecurity Policy Approved by the board, dated, version-controlled. Covers: scope, accountability, risk appetite, update frequency.
Risk Assessment Conducted within the last 12 months. Identified threats, probabilities, consequences and action plan. Signed by responsible person.
Incident Response Plan (IRP) Step-by-step: who is contacted, within how long, how is the incident logged. Incl. contact details for NCSC / sector authority.
Board minutes with cybersecurity agenda At least one set of minutes per year showing the board actively addressed cybersecurity matters and approved measures.
Training certificates (board + CEO) Certificate or documentation of completed NIS2-relevant cybersecurity training for each board member.
Supplier and supply chain security register List of critical suppliers, their security level, contractual requirements and most recent review.
GAP analysis against NIS2 requirements Documented mapping of where you are, where you need to be, and a prioritised action plan with owners and dates.
Art. 21 NIS2 2 — Technical minimum requirements — ask your IT department
Measure Why it is required Done?
Multi-factor authentication (MFA) Protects against password theft — the most common attack vector. Required for all critical systems.
Encrypted communications Email and internal communications with sensitive information must be encrypted in transit and at rest.
Tested backups Regular backups are worthless if recovery has never been tested. Document the most recent test.
Patching and update routine Known vulnerabilities must be addressed systematically. A documented schedule is required.
Access control (least privilege) Employees should only have access to what they need. Documented permissions and offboarding procedure.
Logging and monitoring Logs must be retained long enough to investigate an incident retrospectively.

Good governance practice 3 — Questions to ask at the next board meeting
  • Can we today show the auditor or supervisory authority that we comply with NIS2 requirements — with documentation?
  • Who is responsible for ensuring the incident report is sent to NCSC within 24 hours if something happens tonight?
  • Have we tested our incident response plan in the last 12 months — and do we have minutes documenting it?
  • Which of our suppliers have access to our critical systems, and have we reviewed their security level?
  • Have all board members completed the cybersecurity training required by law?
  • What is our maximum fine risk under NIS2, and is this known to the board?
  • Do we have cyber insurance — and do we know if it actually applies if we are not NIS2 compliant?
Art. 23 NIS2 4 — Reporting timelines for an incident — post this on the wall
Deadline What must be reported To whom
Within 24 hours Early warning — indication that an incident has occurred. Preliminary assessment. NCSC or relevant sector authority
Within 72 hours Incident notification — more detailed information about the event, impact and measures taken. NCSC or relevant sector authority
Within 1 month Final report — complete analysis, root cause, actions taken and lessons learned. NCSC or relevant sector authority

Note: Missed reporting can itself result in fines, regardless of the severity of the incident. NCSC contact: ncsc.gov.ie · reporting@ncsc.gov.ie
