2. Understand the picture

What is NIS2 — really?

Imagine every business in the EU playing the same game against cyber attackers. NIS2 is the new rulebook — and those who don't follow the rules get fined.

📜 The plain-English explanation

NIS2 (Network and Information Security Directive) is an EU directive transposed into Irish law in October 2024 as S.I. No. 322 of 2024. It requires organisations in critical sectors to have a baseline level of cyber protection in place.

It's not about becoming a technology company. It's about having documented procedures, a system for managing incidents, and — most importantly — ensuring that management takes responsibility.

In practice: if your organisation suffers a data breach and you cannot demonstrate that you took security seriously, the NCSC Ireland can direct its enforcement action against you personally.

⚖️ The new element nobody is talking about

Previously, cybersecurity was an IT matter. NIS2 changes that. The management body — your board and senior leadership — is now directly responsible for ensuring that cybersecurity measures actually work, not just that they exist on paper.

This means you as CEO or board member must understand the risks, approve the measures taken and be able to demonstrate what you have done if the NCSC Ireland (or a court) asks. Regulation 32 of S.I. 322/2024 makes this explicit.

What's required?

What do you actually need to do?

NIS2 doesn't demand perfection. It demands that you take responsibility and can show what you've done. Here are the key requirements:

1

Risk management

You need to know what digital risks exist in your business — and have a plan for them. Think: "What happens if our email stops working for a week?"

2

Incident handling

If something goes wrong — a data breach, a virus — you need a process. And you must report it to the authority within 72 hours.

3

Supply chain security

Your suppliers and partners are part of your security. You are responsible for ensuring they also meet reasonable security requirements.

4

Backup and recovery

You must have backups and a plan for how to recover if systems crash or if you're hit by ransomware.

5

Training and procedures

Staff need basic security training. And you need written procedures — not just "we usually do it this way."

6

Management sign-off

The board and CEO must actively approve security work — and document it. It's not enough to delegate it to the IT department and forget about it.

The IT manager has the technical logs. But there is no board decision approving the security measures. No documented risk assessment presented to management. The CEO says: "IT handles those matters." Nobody can show that the board fulfilled its obligations under Article 20.

The outcome: a financial penalty. A formal warning to the CEO. The board chair receives a notice that a temporary ban from management positions was considered. Total cost including fines, consultants, legal handling and lost customers: estimated at €1.5–2 million.

What was missing wasn't the technology. It was the paperwork — and the board's active involvement.

4. Common questions

Questions we hear
all the time.

We're not a tech company — does NIS2 apply to us? ▾
We understand that thinking — and it's common. NIS2 isn't about technology, it's about sectors. If you operate in energy, transport, health, finance, manufacturing or digital infrastructure — and have more than 50 employees — the law applies to you, regardless of whether you have an IT department. The real question is: do you operate in a sector that society depends on? If so, it applies.
We already have antivirus software. Isn't that enough? ▾
Antivirus is like a lock on the front door. It helps — but NIS2 asks whether you have an alarm, cameras, procedures for what happens if someone gets in anyway, and whether the board knows all about it. Technology is part of the answer. Documentation and management accountability are the rest. Most companies have the technology but lack the paperwork and processes.
